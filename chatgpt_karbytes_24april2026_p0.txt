## 🌌 Redshift and Blueshift (the Doppler effect for light)

When a light source moves relative to you, its light gets **stretched or compressed**—just like the pitch of a siren changes as an ambulance passes.

---

## 🔴 Redshift (moving away)

* Light waves get **stretched**
* Wavelength increases → shifts toward **red**
* Frequency decreases

👉 If a galaxy is moving away, all its spectral lines shift slightly toward the red end.

---

## 🔵 Blueshift (moving toward)

* Light waves get **compressed**
* Wavelength decreases → shifts toward **blue**
* Frequency increases

👉 If something is coming toward you, its light shifts toward blue.

---

## 📐 The core relationship

Here’s the key idea scientists use:

[karbytes_annotation: 
z = (λ_subscript(observed) - λ_subscript(emitted)) / λ_subscript(emitted)
]

z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{observed}} - \lambda_{\text{emitted}}}{\lambda_{\text{emitted}}}

* (z > 0) → redshift
* (z < 0) → blueshift
* (z = 0) → no motion relative to you

This compares the **observed wavelength** to the **known “fingerprint” wavelength** of an element.

---

## 🌠 Why this matters in astronomy

### 1) Expanding universe

Most galaxies are redshifted → they’re moving away from us.

This led to the discovery that the universe is expanding, first described by Edwin Hubble.

### 2) Measuring speed

The amount of shift tells us **how fast** something is moving.

* Small shift → slow motion
* Large shift → very fast (even a significant fraction of light speed)

### 3) Detecting exoplanets

* A planet orbiting it
* The planet’s mass and orbit

## 🌌 Using gravitational lensing to measure the universe

Gravitational lensing doesn’t just make pretty arcs—it gives us **measurable geometry** and **timing** we can turn into distances and expansion rates.

---

## ⏱️ 1) Time-delay cosmography (a direct route to expansion)

When a distant quasar is lensed by a foreground galaxy, you often see **multiple images** of the same source.

* Each image’s light travels along a **different path** and through **different gravitational potentials**
* If the quasar varies in brightness, the flickers arrive at slightly **different times** in each image

👉 Measure those **time delays** + model the lens → infer absolute distances → estimate the expansion rate of the universe, often expressed via the **Hubble Constant**.

**Why it’s powerful:** it’s largely **independent** of the traditional “distance ladder,” so it serves as a cross-check.

---

## 📏 2) Lensing as a “cosmic ruler”

Lensing geometry depends on three distances:

* Observer ↔ Lens
* Lens ↔ Source
* Observer ↔ Source

The amount of bending sets a characteristic angular scale (e.g., an Einstein ring). With a good mass model for the lens, that scale becomes a **ruler** for cosmic distances.

---

## 🌠 3) Calibrating other methods

Lensing helps **anchor** or **test** other distance indicators (like supernovae), tightening constraints on cosmic expansion and reducing systematic errors.

---

## 🧠 The big equation behind expansion

At large scales, galaxies follow **Hubble’s Law**:

v = H_0 d

* (v): recession speed (from redshift)
* (d): distance
* (H_0): Hubble constant (current expansion rate)

Lensing helps us determine **(d)** accurately, which sharpens our estimate of **(H_0)**.

---

## 🧩 The “Hubble tension”

Different methods don’t perfectly agree on (H_0):

* Early-universe methods (e.g., cosmic microwave background)
* Late-universe methods (e.g., supernovae, lensing time delays)

Lensing sits in the **late-universe camp** and provides an **independent check**, making it central to the ongoing “Hubble tension” debate.

## 🕰️ 4) Estimating the age of the universe

Once you know (H_0), you can estimate the universe’s age (roughly, the inverse scale of expansion):

* Larger (H_0) → faster expansion → **younger** universe
* Smaller (H_0) → slower expansion → **older** universe

Refined models (including dark energy and matter content) give today’s best estimate of **~13.8 billion years**.

## 🧩 How scientists build gravitational lens models

Turning those beautiful arcs into numbers (distances, masses, (H_0)) requires a **model of the lens** and the **light paths**.

### 1) Start with the data

* High-resolution images (often from Hubble Space Telescope or James Webb Space Telescope)
* Precise positions and shapes of arcs/multiple images
* Spectra → redshifts of **lens** and **source**
* Light curves (for time-delay systems)

### 2) Choose a mass model

You assume a parameterized mass distribution for the lens:

* Smooth components (e.g., an elliptical halo)
* Luminous matter (stars)
* Substructure (clumps of Dark Matter)

Common choices include “isothermal” profiles or more flexible families (e.g., NFW-like halos).

### 3) Ray-trace the light

Using General Relativity, you compute how light bends:

* Map **source plane → image plane**
* Predict where images should appear, their magnification, and distortions

### 4) Fit model to observations

* Compare predicted images to the real data
* Adjust parameters to minimize residuals (often via MCMC or other Bayesian methods)

### 5) Reconstruct the source

* Infer what the **unlensed galaxy/quasar** looks like
* Iterate: better source reconstruction improves the mass model, and vice versa

### 6) For time delays (cosmography)

* Include measured delays between images
* Solve for a key scale called the **time-delay distance**, which depends on the expansion rate

---

## ⚖️ How uncertainties are handled (this is where rigor lives)

There are several major sources of uncertainty—and well-developed strategies to control them:

### 1) Model degeneracies

Different mass distributions can reproduce similar images.

* **Mass-sheet degeneracy**: you can rescale the mass and still fit the images
  **Mitigation:** add independent constraints (stellar kinematics of the lens galaxy, multiple source redshifts, or external data)

### 2) Line-of-sight structure

Matter **between us and the lens** also bends light.

* Small extra deflections can bias distances
  **Mitigation:** map the foreground/background environment; use simulations to estimate and marginalize this effect

### 3) Time-delay measurements

* Quasars vary irregularly; delays require careful curve matching
  **Mitigation:** long monitoring campaigns, multiple analysis pipelines, blind analyses

### 4) Instrument/systematics

* Point spread function (PSF), detector noise, calibration
  **Mitigation:** detailed instrument modeling, cross-checks with different telescopes

### 5) Statistical framework

Most teams use **Bayesian inference**:

* Sample a **posterior distribution** over parameters
* Report uncertainties as credible intervals (not just a single best value)

Short answer: **Yes—on large scales, (H_0) is expected to be the same everywhere.**

## 🌌 Why (H_0) should be the same everywhere

Cosmology is built on the **cosmological principle**:

* The universe is **homogeneous** (same everywhere, on large scales)
* The universe is **isotropic** (looks the same in every direction)

Under this assumption, the expansion rate—captured by the Hubble Constant—is a **global property of spacetime**, not something that varies from place to place.

👉 So in the ideal model:

> Every observer anywhere in the universe would measure the same (H_0), assuming they measure it correctly.

---

## ⚠️ Why measurements can differ locally

Even though (H_0) is globally uniform, **real measurements can vary** because:

### 1) Local gravitational effects (“peculiar velocities”)

* Galaxies don’t just follow expansion—they also move due to nearby gravity
* Example: galaxies in a cluster orbit each other

👉 This adds noise to measurements of expansion

---

### 2) Cosmic structures

* The universe has **clusters, filaments, and voids**
* If you’re in a dense region vs. a void, local measurements can be biased

---

### 3) Measurement methods

Different techniques probe different epochs:

* Early universe (cosmic microwave background) → lower (H_0) (~67)
* Late universe (supernovae, lensing) → higher (H_0) (~73)

👉 This mismatch is called the **Hubble tension**

---

## 🧠 Key distinction

* **(H_0)** → the *true global expansion rate today*
* **Measured (H_0)** → what we infer, affected by:

  * local motion
  * observational limits
  * model assumptions

---

## 🔄 Does (H) vary at all?

Yes—but **in time, not space**.

The Hubble parameter (H(t)):

* Was larger in the early universe
* Changes over cosmic time
* (H_0) is just its value **today**

Think of the universe like a rising loaf of raisin bread:

* The dough expands uniformly (global (H_0))
* But raisins can jiggle locally (peculiar velocities)
